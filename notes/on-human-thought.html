On What Makes a Conversation Real

In response to a recent post on Substack about whether conversations truly happen online.

I agree with the core point: it is absolutely possible to engage one person — or a few people — at a personal level and have an actual conversation. Those moments still exist. They matter. They are rare enough now that we feel them when they happen.

At the same time, I’m not sure technology has eliminated conversation so much as moved parts of it. Some of it has become asynchronous. Some of it has become fragmented. What feels missing to me isn’t just proximity — it’s a space to call our own. A place where thought isn’t immediately compressed into reaction.

Sometimes, too, the most honest thing we can do is stop talking. We might need less commentary and more listening. Less response and more observation. Not everything requires our voice. Some things require our attention.

In the post, there was a line about how the American system was created to allow for flex and growth — for progress. That resonated deeply with me. As a child, that was my understanding of America: imperfect, but structured in a way that allowed for correction.

That belief feels harder to hold today. The environment we live in now rewards immediacy, outrage, and performance more than reflection. We are surrounded by things constantly demanding our attention, and not all of them deserve it. Even the good things, taken in excess, can distort us. Too much noise eventually becomes indistinguishable from none at all.

I believe in personal responsibility. But responsibility only makes sense if we take the time to examine it — otherwise we risk defending positions we haven’t really thought through. Reflection without responsibility becomes drift. The “right thing” only makes sense if we have space to consider what that actually is.

It often feels like we’ve lost shared clarity about what the right thing even means.

I do believe there are shared principles capable of anchoring a society — perhaps even a single enduring value. Integrity. Loyalty. Critical thinking. The discipline of sitting still long enough to examine our own assumptions.

There is power in stillness. There is power in doing nothing for a moment. There is power in thinking before speaking.

I’m also aware that the nature of conversation may continue to change. Artificial intelligence, large language models, and algorithmic feeds are reshaping how ideas move and how people encounter one another. It’s possible that the next form of conversation won’t look like the ones we’re nostalgic for.

But whatever the medium becomes, I suspect the core requirements won’t change: presence, integrity, curiosity, and the willingness to listen without immediately defending.

Thank you for writing the piece. It made me slow down and consider what I actually mean when I say “conversation.”
